André des Freux, S.J.
1554

This short pamphlet is one among many collections of Jesuit theological collections printed at this time. It's particular interest, though, comes from its unique author, place, and time. André de Freux, S.J. (1515-1556) was the first French Jesuit and a close companion of St. Ignatius. He produced the vulgate Latin translation of the Spiritual Exercises that are still the basis of the text we use today. Ignatius trusted him with managing the order's first intellectual initiatives in Messina (1548) and Venice (1550). Later, he served as the first rector of the Roman College (the modern Gregorian University) in 1552 where he strove to prove the order's academic rigor to existing institutions not only with debates and theological presentations (such as what follows), but also through public exhibitions of Latin poetry commenting on issues of the day and through the publication of handbooks on Latin pedagogy.

Theologicae Assertiones collectae ex interpretatione libri Geneseos, quem exposuit hoc anno
Romae

Theological assertions collected from the interpretation of the book of Genesis, which were set forth this year in
Rome

R. Pater Andreas Frusius, professor Theologiae societatis IESV, In Collegio eiusdem societatis.

by the Reverend Father André des Freux, professor of theology of the Society of Jesus, in the college of the same society.

Romae apud Antonium Bladum

Rome: Published under Antonius Bladus.

M. D. L I I I I.

1554

1.

Scripturam sanctam multis de causis necessariam esse generi humano; et tum dignitate, tum certitudine longe praecellere alias omnes disciplinas.
That Sacred Scripture for many reasons is necessary for the human race, and superpasses by far all other disciplines first in dignity and then in truth.

2.

Librum Geneseos omnium, qui extant, librorum antiquissimum, divinitus revelatum, et a Mose scriptam esse: idque multis etiam indiciis cognosci posse.
That the book of Genesis is the most ancient of books which are still extant, was revealed by divine providence, and was written by Moses. And that this is able to be known moreover by many signs.

3.

Fidem mundanae creationis verae philosophiae naturali consonare prorsus, nedum nọṇ repugnare.
That faith in an earthly creation certainly is in harmony with, much less doesn’t oppose, true natural philosophy.

4.

Mundum creatum esse a deo in principio temporis, non ab aeterno vel factum, vel non factum emanare.
That the world was created by God in the beginning of time, neither made from eternity, nor emerging from something made.

5.

In primo temporis momento ex nihilo factam esse materiam primam, duntaxat generali quadam forma, speciales secum admittente, utcumque sustentatam.
That in the first moment of time the first matter was made from nothing, at that moment it had a certain general form, admitting species within itself, howsoever maintained.

6.

Ex materia eadem prima coelum, Elementa, et corpora mixta partim propria, et partim angelica operatione cum temporis progressu extitisse.
From the same first matter, heaven, the elements, and bodies, though particular, were mixed in part, and in part emerged by angelic operation with the progress of time.

7.

Angelos cum suo aevo non ante corpoream naturam creatos esse, ut sapientissimis Theologis placet, et si aliud facile defendi possit.
That angels with their eternity were not created before corporeal nature, as the wisest theologians favor, even if it is something easy to defend.

8.

Solem cum orbibus cunctis coelestibus prima die formatum esse, ac Lucem proprie appellatum.
That the sun with other heavenly spheres was formed on the first day, and properly was called Light.

9.

Firmamenti secundo die facti nomine aetherem, idest, aeris et ignis spatium apte significare.
That on the second day the firmament was made which is called ether, i.e. to signify appropriately a space of air and fire.

10.

Aquas in locum unum congregatas, et maria vocatas continuum facere unum corpus, et, quoad maximas superficiei suae partes, infra littoreos margines naturalem situm obtinere.
That the waters were gathered together into one place, and the seas were called to make one continuous body, and held fast to their natural seat inside the seashores, as far as the greatest parts of their surface.

11.

Partes Universi prius conditas fuisse magis necessarias et aliis condendis servientes, ut lucem, motum, et Angelos. Alioqui ab imperfectioribus ad perfectiores ordinem servatum esse.
That the portions of the earlier Universe had been established rather as clients and servants for the other things being established, such as light, motion, and Angels. In other words, that from less perfect to more perfect order is preserved.

12.

Plantarum, et animalium species perfectas illico fuisse, idque in Vere potius, quam Autumno.
That the species of plants and animals had been at that moment perfect, and it was in Spring rather than Autumn.

13.

Solem quarto die tam perfectionem suam, quam propriam nominis sui rationem consecutum esse.
That on the fourth day the sun reached its perfection as much as the proper reason for its name.

14.

Sydera in signa quidem data esse, non talia tamen, ut per horoscopi natalis rationem certas animi humani propensiones, vel futuros casus portendere possint, nedum effice re, ut genethliaci confingunt, multoque minus Electionibus prodesse, vel obesse.
That the stars truly were given for signs, and yet not the kind that could obtain sure foreshadowings of the human soul through the reckoning of a birth’s horoscope or future events, much less by such to bring anything about, as the palm-readers pretend, and much less to profit or hurt the Elect.

15.

Ex primi hominis productione ostendi animam cuiusque hominis unicam esse, immortalem, a deo creatam, simul et corpori proprio infusam.
That from the production of the first human it is shown that there is a unique soul for each human, that it is immortal, being created by God, and at the same time it is also infused to its own body.

16.

Imaginem dei in diversis hominibus partim similem, partim dissimilem valde esse: nec abs re a nonnullis dici, hominem totum ad imaginem dei factum esse.
That the image of God was somewhat similar in various humans, but even more dissimilar. And that this is said by many from the whole person was made to the image of God.

17.

Mysterium divinae Trinitatis ex verbis creationis quadrupliciter colligi, nec a Iudaeis ratione ulla negari posse.
That the mystery of the divine Trinity from the words of creation is considered in four ways, and that it is not able to be denied by any reason given by the Jews.

18.

Creationis opus coepisse die dominica, ab occasu solis inchoata, et sub feriae sextae vesperam consummatum esse, nec aliam ullam opinionem carere pluribus absurdis.
That it is necessary that creation began on Sunday, from the setting of the sun commencing, and was completed on the evening of the sixth day, and that any other opinion is full of many absurdities.

19.

Sabbatum iam inde fuisse institutum, nec tamen observatum nec observandum ante legis constitutionem.
That from that very moment the Sabbath was instituted, and nevertheless that it was neither observed nor ought it to have been observed before the constituting of the Law.

20.

Sic Deum die illo septimo ab omni opere quievisse, ut, licet operetur semper, nihil tamen novi facere videatur.
That God on that seventh day had rested from every labor so that, though he always works, he appears nonetheless to make nothing new.

21.

Opera sex dierum a deo sic dici bona, ut fatendum sit, mundum et, quam optimus fieri potuerit, factum esse, et unicum esse quod Aristoteles debilissime probavit.
That the works of the six days were said to be good by God so that it must be confessed that the world had been made as good as it could possible be and that and was unified, something that Aristotle very poorly proved.

22.

Paradisum terrestrem certum locum fuisse, et adhuc extare, et probabiliter in confinio Arabiae, Mesopotamiae, et Syriae esse, licet in accessum.
That the earthly Paradise had been a fixed place and still exists, and likely is in the boundaries of Arabia, Mesopotia, and Syria, although inaccessible.

23.

Adamum extra Paradisum creatum dici probabilius. Evam vero in Paradiso post sextum diem, nec ambos eodem eiectos die, quo formatos.
That Adam more likely is said to have been created outside Paradise; but Eve inside Paradise after the sixth day. And that both were ejected on the same day that they were formed.

24.

Arborem quidem vitae habuisse naturalem vim suo nomini respondentem: alteram vero scientiae boni et mali, ab eventu potius, quam ante peccatum sic cognominatam.
That the tree of life really had a natural power according to its name. And that the other tree, of knowledge of good and evil, was named this more from the consequence than from something outside the realm of sin.

25.

Iustitiam originalem fuisse donum supernaturale gratiae gratis datae, coniunctum cum dono gratiae iustificantis.
That original justice had been a supernatural gift of grace freely given, coupled with a gift of justifying grace.

26.

Probabile admodum esse, quod filius dei per se, vel per Angelum primis parentibus, aliisque antiquis patribus in forma humana familiariter apparebat, ad insinuandum mysterium futurae incarnationis.
That it is highly probable that the Son of God, through Himself or through an angel, appeared familiarly to our first parents and other ancient Fathers in a human form, in order to foreshadow the mystery of His future incarnation.

27.

Sacramentum matrimonii iam tunc institutum esse, et ex vi verborum, fornicationem, multitudinem uxorum, Divortium, excepta fornicationis causa, et eo facto coniugium supposititium prohiberi.
That the sacrament of matrimony was then at that time instituted, and, by the strength of His words, that fornication, polygamy, divorce (except for instances of fornication), and, when this was done, the addition of another spouse were prohibited.

28.

Tentationis Diabolum authorem, Serpentem organum extitisse: ac utrique aliquid poenae, toti generi commune, quod prius non aderat, fuisse inflictum.
That the devil, the author of temptation, took the form of a serpent; and that something of their indemnification, common to the whole species, which had not been present before, had been inflicted.

29.

Deum neque directe, nec indirecte peccati illius extitisse causam, neque gloriam ipsius citra illud minorem futuram fuisse.
That God, neither directly nor indirectly, had been the cause of that sin, and that after that sin His glory would not have been any less.

30.

Foeminam, simpliciter loquendo, gravius, quam virum, peccasse.
That the woman, to speak plainly, had sinned more gravely than the man.

31.

Peccatum originale, si ad primariam peccati significationem spectemus, apte vocari peccatum primi parentis. Verum si ad alteram significationem intueamur, qua frequentius uti consuevimus, quiddam esse, quod ex peccato primi parentis, tanquam ex causa, realiter in singulos filios manat.
That original sin, if we look to the first signification of sin, is fitting called the sin of the first parent. But if we turn to another signification, which we are accustomed to use more frequently, is the condition which remains in reality on each descendant from the sin of the first parent, as much as it is the cause.

32.

Homines omnes, si Adam non peccasset, per totum orbem iucundissime victuros fuisse: non tamen tutos ab omni peccato, nec sine aliquo proprio cuiusque merito aeterna gloria donandos.
That all humans, if Adam had not sinned, would have lived most joyfully through the whole world; yet nevertheless they would need to be saved from every sin and not without some merit, proper to each, of eternal glory.

33.

Abstinentiam et delectum ciborum in Ecclesia deo placere, et Evangelicam veram libertatem non imminuere, sed augere contra Deum Lutheranorum, Ventrem.
That abstinence from and distinction among foods in the Church is pleasing to God, and that they do not diminish true evangelical freedom, but increase it, contrary to the God of the Lutherans: their Belly.

34.

Erubescendi causam potissimum fuisse, et esse naturalem concupiscentiae homine indignae cognitionem.
That it chiefly was cause of shame, and is the natural reflection on a concupiscence unworthy of the human person.

35.

Tentatorem Evae fuisse potius Luciferum quam alium daemonem, et huc referri quod ait, Diabolum a principio esse homicidam.
That Lucifer was the tempter of Eve, rather than any other demon, and that it refers to him when it says, “the devil, from the beginning, is the killer of men.”

36.

Eundem supremum fuisse omnium angelorum non improbabiliter dici posse, ac peccasse, quod dedignaretur adorare praemonstratam Christi humanitatem. Christum quoque deum et hominem futurum fuisse, licet Adam non peccasset. Problematice haec omnia disputationis causa, non studio novitatis.
That it is can be said, not improbably, that this same one was greatest, and that he had sinned, because he refused to adore the humanity of Christ when it was presaged. That Christ also would have to become God and man, though Adam had not sinned. All these things are set forth hypothetically for reason of disputation, not love of novelty.

37.

Inter mulieris poenas non poni multiplicandos partus, sed dolores partuum, eosque esse naturales.
That, among the penalties assigned to women, their deliveries were not multiplied, but the pains of delivery; and these were natural.

38.

Virum in uxorem etiam ante peccatum, peculiare dominium habuisse, sed alterum postea, medium quodammodo inter paternum atque despoticum accessisse.
That, even before sin, a man had had a special dominion over his wife, but afterwards, another, somewhat between paternal and despotic oversight, was added.

39.

Dominium in animalia, naturali iure debitum, commissa culpa excidisse, Licet remanserit usus aliquis ac terror.
That the dominion over animals, given by natural law, fell away after the crime had been committed, though a kind of use and terror remained.

40.

Non solum terram, cui maledictum est, sed reliquam omnem quoque creaturam erga hominem post peccatum, ut prius, benignam non esse, idque remanente naturae rerum, sed mutata gubernatione.
That not only the earth, which is cursed, but all the remainder of creation as well is not benign to the human person after sin, as it had been before, and that governance over the things of nature remains, but it has changed.

41.

Propter idem peccatum sic humano generi universo poenas infligi; ut nemo prorsus a reatu huius poenae immunis fuerit, vel futurus sit.
That, because of this same sin, penalties were inflicted upon all humankind, so that no one after has been, or would be, immune from the guilty status that merits this punishment.

42.

Non debuisse tolli poenas seu poenalitates eiusmodi per Christi meritum, dum in hac mortali vita vivimus, sed gratiam relictis magis, quam sublatis illis, superabundare.
That it had not been necessary to lift these punishments or penalties in such a manner through Christ’s merit, while we live out this mortal life, but that grace abounds more on those remaining than on those carried off.

43.

Poenas tamen primis parentibus irrogatas non ita singulis hominibus fieri communes, quin liceat, deoque gratius sit, aliter quam manibus laborare, et matrimonium cum aerumnis suis declinare.
That, nevertheless, the penalties imposed upon the first parents, not as individual human beings, were made common, not that it would not be possible and more pleasing to God in any other way than laboring with our hands and declining marriage with its hardships.

44.

Angelos ad paradisi aditum custodiendum collocatos Cherubin fuisse potius, ut scriptura vocat, quam alios sic cognominatos*, ut subditos. Ex cunctis enim ordinibus decere nonnullos mitti: salva nihilominus Beati Dionysii authoritate.

*MS = cognomimatos.
That the angels placed to guard the entrance to Paradise had been the Cherubim, as Scripture calls them, rather than others thus named, as substitutes. For it is appropriate for some from all orders to be sent, without impinging upon no less an authority than Blessed Dionysius.

45.

Hominem in statu corruptae naturae ne morale quidem bonum nedum meritorium ad salutem, vel gratiam posse operari. Neque tamen opera ex genere suo bona, proprie loquendo peccata esse.
That the human person in this state of corrupt nature was not able to work anything morally good, much less meriting salvation or grace. And that, nevertheless, works on their own are not good, to speak properly they are sins.

46.

Deum statim post parentum lapsum, ex immensa sua misericordia, remedium fidei, salutatis, et ipsis, et posteritati providisse.
That God, immediately after the fall of our parents, from his immense mercy, had provided the remedy of faith and salvation both to themselves and their posterity.

47.

Nihil absurdi esse, si dicatur Coniugii usus in paradiso terrestri coepisse, et futurus fuisse, quum nihil penitus vel libidinis vel turpitudinis habeat.
That there is nothing absurd if someone says that the use of conjugal rights had begun in the earthly paradise and that it was meant to be, since it would have nothing in it of lust or shame.

48.

Deum nunquam creaturum fuisse hominem, nisi iustitia originali adiecta, ne ut monstrum foret in natura.
That God never would have created man unless justice had been added to the original so that there would be nothing monstrous in nature.

49.

Cain et aliorum adultorum reprobationem factam non fuisse, quoad ultimum effectum, qui est damnatio, absque propria ipsorum malitia.
That no condemnation had been passed against Cain and other inveterates, because the result was final, i.e. damnation, apart from their own particular malice.

50.

Ex dictis ad Cain liberum hominis arbitrium constare, per se quidem ad humanas tantum actiones, ex genere bonas vel malas, cum gratia vero ad meritorias quoque aeternae salutis se extendens, salva nihilominus et hominis dignitate contra Pelagium, et tam divina praescientia, quam gratiae integritate, contra Lutherum.
That the free will of the human persons accords with the things said to Cain, indeed through it to all human actions, whether good or evil in their kind, since grace extends even to things meriting eternal salvation, just as much as the saved dignity of the human person, contrary to the opinion of Pelagius, and so accords with the Providence of God as much the integrity of grace, contrary to the opinion of Luther.

51.

Deum non respexisse ad Cain, et munera eius ob peccatum, idque non tam avaritiae, vel negligentiae, quam infidelitatis fuisse, videri ex Pauli verbis.
That God had not shown respect for Cain and his gifts on account of his sin, and that it seems not so much a sin of avarice or neglect as infidelity, according to the words of Paul.

52.

Respicientem vero ad Abel primum, deinde et ad munera eius: significasse opera sibi non placere, nisi grata sit operans persona, quod non praestat fides sine charitate, contra Lutheranos.
That, on the other hand, He looks to Abel first, and then to his gifts signified that works do not please Him, unless the person working is pleasing, because faith is not manifest without charity, contrary to the opinion of the Lutherans.

53.

Per punitionem Cain excommunicatorum statum infoelicissimum pulchre assignari, cum omnium tum maxime haereticorum.
That the completely dreadful state of the excommunicated is beautifully shown in the punishment of Cain, since he is the greatest of all heretics.

54.

Sententiam in Cain pronunciatam, et temporalem et aeternam complecti damnationem. Aliquos itaque a deo plane reprobari, ob insignem perversitatem, quales potissimum coniicere licet esse haeresiarchas.
That the sentence pronounced against Cain is fulfilled with both temporal and eternal damnation. And so others are clearely reproved by God, on account of their gross perversity, especially such as those who might be lumped together as the heresiarchs.

55.

Prohibitam certis de causis occisionem Cain nihil obstare, quominus scelerati homines praesertim haeretici obstinati, tanquam omnium perniciosissimi, gladio potestatis, non temere portando tolli de medio debeant.
That the prohibition against murder for fixed reasons did not stop Cain at all, since wicked men certainly are obstinate heretics, in much as they are the most pernicious of all men, and they ought to be put out of the way by sword of the sovereign, not by any that happens to be lying around.

56.

Cum dicatur Adam genuisse filium ad imaginem et similitudinem suam, non minus hominem, quam aliud quoduis animal generantem univocum censeri posse. licet animarum utriusque longe diversa sit natura et origo.
That, when Adam is said to have borne a son according to his image and likeness, it can be thought that man, no less less than any other animal, engenders offspring that share a name, though the nature and origin of each of the animals is much more diverse.

57.

Scripturam haec verba, Et mortuus est, inculcantem, nobis insinuare deum non fecisse mortem, sed hanc potius tam voluntati eius, quam naturae ipsius constitutioni esse contrariam. Unde etiam innaturalis dici possit.
That Scripture, when it emphasizes these words “And he died” (Gen 5:5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 20, 27, 31), reveals to us that God did not make death, but rather that this is as contrary to His will as to the nature of His constitution. And so it is also possible to say that is unnatural.

58.

Per filios die ducentes filias hominum uxores non posse angelos intelligi.
That angels are not able to be understood through the sons of God taking as wives the daughters of men.

59.

Ex hoc loco apparere rationem, cur fidelium cum infidelibus prohibitum sit coniugium, nec minus forsitan piorum cum impiis, vel malis christianis vitandum esse.
That from this passage appears the reason why the faithful are prohibited from marrying the faithless, and no less, perhaps, the pious with the impious, or the necessity of avoiding evil Christians.

60.

Centum et viginti annorum spatium poenitentiae, ante dilu[v]ium non vitae humanae praescriptum esse.
That a space of 120 years, before the flood, had been proscribed for penance, not for human life.

61.

Hanc vero communiter. 70. vel ad summum. 80. annorum periodo finiri.
That, otherwise, in general, the end had been fixed at 70 or at maximum 80 years.

62.

Lamech duas uxores accepisse, non narrari a scriptura tanquam detestandum, et legi naturae contrarium,
That Lamech had taken on two wives is not narrated by Scripture as if it must be denounced and contrary to natural law.

63.

Corruptionem universae carnis non ex reprobatione divina, vel solo peccato originali, sed ex electione mala contigisse, ut in coniugiis supradictis.
That the corruption of all flesh comes not from divine condemnation or original sin alone, but from our bad choices as in the marriages cited above.

64.

Omnes animalium propagabilium species in Arca Noe fuisse. Longe tamen pauciores quam nunc sint, propter specierum commixtionem, nec in illam sine angelorum ministerio introductas.
That all species of animals that propagate had been in Noah’s Ark. Yet there were far fewer than there are now, on account of the mixing of species. Nor were they led into the ark without the ministry of angels.

65.

Animalia in arca mitia omnia fuisse, et vixisse herbis, idque miraculo. A natura enim plaeraque saeva et carnivora esse, quasi ad daemonum et hominum malorum specimen atque abominationem facta.
That all the animals in the ark had been gentle and had lived on grasses, and that it was by a miracle. For very many animals are savage and carnivores by nature as though they were made as an abomination and token of the demons and evil men.

66.

Diluvium universale neque vi astrorum (ut quidam volunt) neque per causas solas naturales fieri potuisse, neque supercoelestes aquas defluxisse, neque terrestres vacuos meatus reliquisse, sed partem äeris rarefactam, et alteram in aquas versam per angelos fuisse.
That the universal flood could not have been caused by the power of the stars (as some wish) or through natural causes alone. Neither the waters above the heavens had flowed down, nor had those on earth left their courses abandoned, but angels had made a portion of the air rarified and turned another portion into waters.

67.

Terram diluvio infoecundiorem esse factam, ob eamque licet non solam, neque praecipuam fortasse causam, permissum esse hominibus esum carnium.
That the earth had been made less fecund by the flood, because it had not been left alone, and the chief reason perhaps was that it had been permitted for men to eat flesh.

68.

Arcum coelestem et ante diluvium fuisse, et naturaliter fieri, et dum fit signum esse diluvii non futuri per naturalem rationem: sed non quoad supernaturalem, nisi deus promisisset nunquam fore, signumque esse voluisset.
That the rainbow had existed even before the flood, and occurred naturally, and at the time became a sign that there would be no future flood through natural reason: but not because it is supernatural, unless God had promised that it would never be, and had wished it to be a sign.

69.

Maledictionem Cham ab haeresticis incurri, eoque graviorem, quod Ecclesiae praelatos patribus magis reverendos, petulantissime derideant, quorum vitia siqua sint, deberent tegere, ut Sem et Iaphet patris sui verenda.
That Ham incurred a curse by his heretical acts, and therefore more serious because, in the most petulant manner possible, they make fun of the Church’s prelates who ought to be respected even more than fathers, whose sins, if there are any, out to be covered, as Shem and Japeth revere their father.

60. [70.]

Per Chanaan maledictum ipsum cham intelligi, non in sua tantum persona, sed in progenie quoque puniendum, neminem autem puniri ob delictum alterius iuxta veram poenae rationem.
That the same curse of Ham is understood to apply to Canaan, not only in his person, but also to be punished in his progeny, although no one is punished because of the fault of another according to the real reckoning of punishment.

71.

Aedificantibus turrim Babel diversa diversis idiomata, per species ab Angelis in ditas, dari potuisse, multa alia tamen postea humanitus inventa, vel ex gentium inter se commertiis confusa extitisse. Sed longe maius miraculum in Apostolis factum esse, singulis omnia distincte callentibus.
That it had been possible for those diverse people building the tower of Babel to speak in diverse idioms, throughout the species by angels for enriching us, yet many others afterward were invented from humans, or those that existed were confused by the mixing of nations among each other. But something far more miraculous was done among the Apostles, when each distinctly understood everything.

72.

Abraham non tunc primum, quum scriptura narrat, sed et ante et post, tum per fidem, tum per opera in fide, et ex charitate facta deo placuisse, ac fuisse iustum.
That Abraham was not just at first, since Scripture says, but both before and after, at first through faith, and then through works in faith, both his works by their charity had pleased God and he was just.

73.

Circuncisionem in signum Iustitiae fidei Abrahae, et alias multas ob causas, esse institutam.
That circumcision was instituted as a sign of justification of faith for Abraham, and for many other reasons.

74.

Causam, cur Sara promisso filio ridens, fuerit reprehensa: non itidem Abraham, videri esse: quod illa de potentia promittentis, hic de voluntate solum adhuc haesitaret.
That the reason why Sarah laughed at the promise of a son would have been her reluctance. It did not seem to be likewise with Abraham because she hesitated on account of her ability to produce what was promised, he only on account of his will at the time.

75.

Abraham et Isaac uxores suas sorores appellando, non prostituisse, nec tantasse Deum, immo si aliter fecissent, tentaturos potius fuisse.
Abraham and Isaac, by calling their wives their sisters, did not dishonor them, nor did they put God to the test, inasmuch as if they had done otherwise, they themselves would run risk.

76.

Saram veram fuisse sororem Abrahae, non ex fratre neptem, nec illicito matrimonio ipsi iunctam.
That Sarah truly had been a sister of Abraham, not a niece from his brother, nor joined to him by an illicit marriage.

77.

Abraham, et alios Patres divino consilio apud diversas gentes peregrinatos esse: ut vel sic agnoscere verum deum possent, et ad Christi fidem disponi.
That Abraham and the other Fathers, by divine counsel, had wandered among many nations, so that they might thus recognize the true God and be disposed to faith in Christ.

78.

Promissum dei de non evertenda civitate propter decem iustos, affirmantis esse potius se ita facturum, quam negantis reperiri illos posse apud Sodomorum civitates, ut voluit Augustinus. De his autem civitatibus solum dictum esse.
That God’s promise to not destroy the city for the sake of ten just men is Him affirming that it would be so rather than Him denying that he was able to find such men among the citizens of Sodom, as Augustine wanted. Still, it is said only about these citizens.

79.

Loth a Beato Petro iustum nominari, et nihilominus in multis peccasse, neque repugnare haec sibi invicem.
That Lot is named just by Blessed Peter, though he still had sinned in many things and had not resisted these things in turn.

80.

Sodomitas non temporalem tantum, sed aeternum quoque ignem meruisse, quamvis essent tempora ignorantiae, ut ait Paulus. neque propterea quod utroque modo puniti sint, derogatur scripturae neganti deum bis idem punire.
That Sodom had merited not only temporal, but even eternal fire, although these were times of ignorance, as Paul says. And it is not denied, therefore, that there were punished in each way, when scripture doesn’t say that God punishes the same thing twice.

81.

Per Agar et filium pulchre significari Lutheranorum sectam cum sua prole, penitus servilem, accersita sibi Ventris et Veneris necessitate. Et quod Ecclesiae vere dominae subiici recuset, populoque vere libero insultet, alius peccati servus, merito eiectam esse matrem cum filio et in deserto continue errare,
That the sect of Lutherans with its offshoots is beautifully signified through Hagar and her son, being servile at its core, arraigned under necessity to their belly and Venus. And because it refuses to submit to its true mistress, the Church, and insults the truly free people, it is another slave of sin. The mother with her son were deservedly cast out and perpetually wonder in the desert.

82.

Utrumque verum esse, deum et neminem tentare (ut ait Iacobus) et nihilominus tentasse Abraham, ut hic asseritur, aliosque innumerabiles tentare. Rursus fidelibus et optandum esse ut tententur, et orandum tamen, ne in tentationem inducantur,
That both are true, both that God tempts no man (as Jacob says) and, just the same, that He had tempted Abraham, as this is claimed, and tempts countless others. Again, the faithful should not wish to be tempted and, further, should pray that they be not led into temptation.

83.

Abraham ad filium immolandum perrexisse non affectu solum obsequii divini, qui facti considerationem posset avertere: sed etiam certa quadam deliberatione adductum (ut innuit Paulus) quae meritum tamen auxerit, potius quam diminuerit.
That Abraham proceeded to sacrifice his son, not only in love of divine obedience, which wasn’t able to reject the consideration of the deed, but also under a certain deliberation (as Paul intimates) which, moreover, increased the merit accounted to him, rather than diminished it.

84.

Non licere petere signum a deo, nisi ut fecit servus Abrahae, per modum orantis et optantis scire dei voluntatem, vel ex divino instinctu. Alioqui tentari deum, vel praesumi eius donum ut lutheranorum se habet fides.
That it is not allowed to seek a sign from God, except as the servant of Abraham did, through a manner of praying and wishing to know the will of God, or by divine intimation. In any other way, God is tempted or makes presumption upon His gift, as faith holds itself among the Lutherans.

85.

Nomine concubinarum intelligi uxores Abrahae, ut aliis in locis fornicationem vero, etiam simplicem, simpliciter prohibitam semper fuisse, contra Lutheranorum libidinem, Exemplo Iudae malo male fultam.
That those going under the name of concubines are understood to be the wives of Abraham, as in other places fornication, when understood plainly, plainly had always been prohibited, contrary to the lust of the Lutherans, which is poorly supported by the bad example of Judah.

86.

Iacob emendis primogenitis, et praeripienda Benedictione fratris sui, nec non et in gregibus, deluso socero, acquirendis, ab omni etiam mendacii officiosi peccato immunem dici posse, et potius debere.
That Jacob, by purchasing the rights of the first-born, by snatching first the blessing of his brother, and especially in acquiring the flocks by the deception of his father-in-law, is not only able to be, but rather ought, to be said to be immune from every crime of ingratiating lying.

87.

Ex voto Iacob liquere, quod Vota piorum simpliciter, aut etiam cum aliqua conditione facta, Deo palceant, invitis, soli Diabolo se devoventibus, Lutheranis.
That it is clear from the vow of Jacob that the vows of the pious plainly, or even deeds accompanied by some condition, please God, although they are not pleasing to Lutherans who are only devoted to the Devil.

88.

Septennalem Iacob servitutem pro Rachele obtinenda (si nondum obtinebat, ut nonnulli putant) levem illi non immerito visam esse, idque etiam naturae, ac virtutis esse rationem potius, quam appetitum sequentis; quod maxime ostendit Paulus, de levitate tribulationis, et gloriae pondere inter se comparandis.
That Jacob’s seven-years of service to obtain Rachel (if he had not yet obtained her, as a few think) seem, and that it was on account of nature and virtue rather than following his desire; a fact that Paul best laid out, by comparing the lightness of his tribulation with the weight of glory.

89.

Laban ab idololatria, Rachelem a furto, Dinam a curiositate, Simeon, et Levi ab iniquitate non videri posse excusari.
That it does not seem possible to excuse Laban from idolatry, Rachel from theft, Dinah from curiosity, Simeon and Levi from iniquity.

90.

Ioseph narrantem somnia sua, licet occasionem daret fratribus invidiae, et sceleris secuti, a scandalo tamen, et peccato liberum fuisse.
That Joseph, when he told his dreams, though he gave occasion for this brothers’ envy and crime that followed, still was free from scandal and sin.

91.

Dicente Iacob se descensurum ad filium Ioseph in infernum, et aliis ex locis, convinci Hebraeos, et Lutheranos vocem Hebraicam pro, Sepulchro, semper accipi volentes, et nusquam fieri mentionem de inferno in testamento veteri.
That, when Jacob says that he would descend to their brother Joseph in the lower world, and from other passages, the Hebrews were convicted, as are the Lutherans who always want to take the Hebrew saying as “the tomb,” and there never was mention of the lower world in the Old Testament.

92.

Eundem non fuisse nec re, nec nomine dominum Ioseph, et Sacerdotem Heliopoleos, cuius duxit in uxorem filiam: licet aliqui contendant.
That the master of Joseph and the priest of Heliopolis, whose daughter Joseph took for his wife, were not the same person, in fact or name, although some say otherwise.

93.

Aegyptiis Sacerdotes suos de publico sustentantibus, et terras eorum vendi nolentibus argui: ut religionem, ita Sacerdotum stipendia, et immunitates ad ius naturale referri posse.
That it is proven that the Egyptians sustained their priest on the public dole and did not want their land to be sold. So religious custom such as priests’ stipends and their immunities are able to be traced back to natural law.

94.

Ioseph divino impulsu erga fratres ita se gessisse, ut refert scriptura. Alioqui a peccato excusari non posse, cum illos, tum patrem maxime affligendo.
That Joseph, by divine impulse, had borne himself in this way against his brothers, as scripture relates. In other respects, it is not possible to excuse him from sin when he caused them, and then most of all his father, such distress.

95.

Augurandi genus, quod simulavit Ioseph, atque aliud quodvis, esse illicitum, nec minus superstitiosum esse, quod Doctor quidam defendendi causa adducit, Exemplum de Astrologicis.
The type of augury, which Joseph feigned, and any other type is not licit, nor is it any less superstitious because a certain learned man adds this as reason to defend it as an example for the astrologers.

96.

Ex iureiurando, quo astrinxit Iacob filium Ioseph, et hic fratres, ut Abraham etiam servuum, ostendi iuramentum per Deum, et creaturas, asserendo, promittendo, vel imprecando, non esse illicitum.
That by the oath whereby Jacob bound his son Joseph and brothers, as Abraham had also bound his servant, we are given an example of an oath that declares, promises, or invokes God and his creatures that is not illicit.

97.

Omnium Benedictionum Iacob sensus principalem esse allegoricum, et in benedictione Iudae certum praefiniri tempus, quo venit Messias Iesus Christus, nihil obstantibus Iudaeorum quibuslibet commentis.
That the principal meaning of all of Jacob’s blessing is allegorical and that he establishes a fixed time in the blessing of Judah, when Jesus Christ, the Messiah, would come, and whatever the Jews might say about it opposes nothing.

98.

Luctum mortuorum in veteri, ac novo etiam testamento laudari potius, quam damnari: moderatum tamen, et fidei consonum esse oportere. Itidem de cura propriae Sepulturae, cum plurimum referre possit.
That the mourning over the dead is praised, rather than condemned, in the Old as well as the New Testament. Still, it is proper for it to be moderate and consonant with the faith. It ought to be the same in taking care of one’s own funeral, since it can represent so much.

99.

Ex verbis Ioseph fratrum timorem mitigantis, impie asseri a Lutheranis, Deum peccatorum authorem esse, quasi omnia pro arbitrio suo gerentem, posita in secundis causis omnimoda necessitate.
That from the words of Joseph lessening the fear of his brothers it is impiously asserted by the Lutherans that God is the author of sins, as though he bore all things upon his will, placing all sorts of things, by necessity, in the position of secondary causes.

100.

Quicquid praeterea in expositione huius libri a praeceptore meo R. Patre Andrea Frusio traditum est, pro virili defendam: non tam propriae cuiusquam tuendae opinionis causa, quam excutiendae veritatis, ad gloriam dei, qui est in secula benedictus. Amen.
Moreover, my teacher, the Reverend Father André des Freux handed down whatever is expounded in this book and I will defend it vigorously, not so much to safeguard some particular opinion as to examine the truth, for the glory of God who is blessed forever. Amen.
Finis.
The End.
